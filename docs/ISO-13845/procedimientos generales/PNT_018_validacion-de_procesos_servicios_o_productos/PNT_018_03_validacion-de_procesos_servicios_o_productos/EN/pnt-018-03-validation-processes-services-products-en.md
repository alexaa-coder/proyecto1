## **1. Aim**
The objective of this procedure is to establish the general validation policy applied by Spika Tech, hereinafter SPIKA, for the products it supplies.
## **2. Scope**
This procedure covers everything from the definition of user requirements to product validation. This document defines the general scope of validation and the activities involved,
although the strategy and planning of specific activities will be included in the protocols, and their execution and results will be documented in the corresponding reports.
## 3. Responsibilities and Departments Affected
It is the responsibility of all SPIKA employees to collaborate and manage product validation activities.
The Technical Manager and the Research and Development staff are responsible for supervising the execution and compliance of this procedure.

## **4. Definitions and Acronyms** • Acceptance criteria: Product specifications and acceptance/rejection criteria, such as
the acceptable quality level, with an associated sampling plan, that will be necessary to make the decision to accept or reject a product.
## • Hardware and Software Installation Qualifications: Documents the installation,
configuration, and qualification instructions for hardware and software, including middleware, to demonstrate that all of these items can perform the functions required
by the application software.
## • Operational Qualification (OQ): establishes, by objective evidence, that the equipment
or instrument is capable of operating repeatedly within the determined limits and meeting the specifications.
## • Performance Qualification (PQ): establishes, based on scientific evidence, that the
equipment or instrument is capable of delivering effective and reproducible results according to the approved process and product specifications.
## • Validation: According to the FDA, it is "the establishment of documented evidence that
provides a high degree of assurance that a specific process will consistently produce a product that meets its predetermined specifications and quality attributes."
According to European standards, validation is "the obtaining of evidence, in accordance with Good Manufacturing Practices, that any procedure, process,
equipment, activity, or system actually produces the intended result." Validation refers to processes, systems, and methods.
## **5. Procedure** **5.1. Generalities**
Validation is the collection and evaluation of documentation from the design phase of a process through commercial production, establishing scientific evidence that a process is
capable of ensuring product quality.
An effective validation process contributes significantly to ensuring product quality.

An important point to consider in the validation exercise will be the changes. Each relevant change within the product must be reviewed and evaluated to determine whether or not it
requires revalidation, as well as periodic quality reviews.
For each validation exercise, a validation protocol must be duly reviewed and approved before its execution, and it must be performed by trained personnel. The trained personnel will collect
all data, as well as any incidents or nonconformities detected, to issue a final judgment and declare the validation exercise as satisfactory or not in the corresponding report. Where
applicable, after detecting nonconformities, corrective actions will be initiated as necessary, and the compliance status of the validation exercise will be assessed.
All generated documentation will be archived in a secure location in the Quality Archive.

**5.3.3.** **Production processes**
Process validation requires that the process is carried out using qualified facilities, systems, and equipment, that the corresponding methods have been validated, that work procedures
are in place, and that personnel have been trained in these procedures.
The validation strategy is reflected in the specific validation protocol. Acceptance criteria must be defined in advance, documented, and justified in the validation protocols, based on
the results of the AR prepared in each case.
Process validation must be completed prior to product distribution and sale.
A corresponding validation protocol will be established for each process, defining the type of validation, the number of batches to be validated, the number and quantity of samples to be
collected, and the analyses to be performed. If statistically, a result of one or two batches to be validated is obtained, the validation must be performed considering three batches.
In the concurrent and prospective validation approach, process validation is typically carried out with a number of consecutive batches established based on the results of the AR.
Deviations from this system may be permitted under justified circumstances.
Likewise, a validation may concern only the manufacturing or packaging phase because the modification requiring validation only affects one of the phases. This will be clearly defined in
the corresponding validation protocol.
They all have a common format in which the following points are defined:
## • Aim. • Responsibilities. • Scope. • Teams. • Definitions. • Staff training.

## **5.5. Re-validation**
Validation is not a one-time activity, but rather a continuous one. To achieve this, a system must be defined that detects unplanned deviations from the designed process, thus enabling
control to be maintained or improved and process and product variability to be reduced.
Continuous process verification through monitoring and trend analysis will determine whether process changes are necessary. All of these efforts should identify process variability
and identify potential improvements.
