# 2. Definitions / acronyms
**Risk:** combination of the probability of occurrence of harm and the severity of that
harm.
**Risk management:** systematic application of management policies, procedures and
practices to the tasks of analysing, evaluating, controlling and monitoring risk.
**Injury:** physical lesion, health damages, property or environmental damages.
**Harm:** Health damage, including the damage that can occur from loss of product
quality or availability.
**Hazard:** The potential source of harm.
**Hazardous Situation:** circumstance in which people, property or the environment are
exposed to one or more hazards (ISO 14971)
**Adverse event:** product defect (i.e. malfunction or failure, deterioration in
characteristics or performance, or inadequacy of labelling or of instructions for use)
that, directly or indirectly, has led or might have led to serious consequences, namely deterioration in the state of health of the patient, user or another person. Also called
an incident.
**Severity:** A measure of the possible consequences of a hazard.
**Probability of Occurance:** Rank the probability of a failure occurring during the
conception, manufacturing and commercialization of the product
**Risk Assessment:** Set of operations that allows the evaluation of the potential harm
(damage) or injury.
**Risk Analysis:** Study that aims to determine the possible risks that may occur during
the operational phase of a product.
**Risk Control:** Application of a set of operations that allows evaluation, control and
monitoring of risks associated with a particular product or process in order to ensure that the identified risks do not occur or has a very low probability.
**Residual Risk:** Risk remaining after risk control measures have been taken.
Potential Hazard Identification and Assessment Team (PHIAT): Representatives of several functions that contribute to risk evaluation according to their specific
knowledge.
**RMT:** Risk Management Team
**RI:** Risk Index
**S:** Severity
**P:** Probability of Occurrence
**RMP:** Risk Management Plan
**RMR:** Risk Management Report

# 6. Risk acceptability
The risk is estimated for each potential harm, resulting in the combination of the following factors: Severity (S) and Probability of Occurrence (P) multiplying the
different weights for those factors to obtain the RPN (Risk Priority Number).
RPN = P x S Each risk estimated must be systematically evaluated, considering that risks should
be reduced as far as possible without adversely affecting the benefit-risk ratio.
Therefore, even if the risk is acceptable, it is necessary to implement measures to bring the risk to its lowest level. Note that is not only enough to bring the risk into
the green area, but the risk management team should also reduce the risk continuously.
In cases, the risk is in a land above the acceptable, but for which is possible to coexist with, related to the benefits associated and the impracticability of reducing it, it
can be considered a tolerable risk.
The risk assessment will be used for both initial risk assessment and residual risk assessment.

# 8. Analysis, assessment and risk management
Risk management is an essential stage in the life cycle of a medical device. This analysis highlights the potential hazardous phenomena associated with the device, as well as
identifying the underlying causes.
This assessment determines whether, or not, the risks identified are considered acceptable.
Risk acceptability criteria may vary depending on the specific medical device and the applicable regulations.
The next phase consists of implementing risk control measures. Appropriate actions are taken to reduce the identified risks to their lowest possible level. These measures may
include design improvements, the addition of protective devices or the provision of clear warnings to users.
Once these measures have been implemented, the residual risk is assessed. This is the level of risk that remains once the risk reduction measures have been applied. This assessment is
followed by a benefit/risk analysis, which examines whether the potential benefits of the medical device outweigh the residual risks identified.
Finally, an overall assessment of the acceptability of all residual risks is carried out. This assessment considers the individual residual risks as well as their overall contribution to the
safety and performance of the medical device. It determines whether the level of residual risk is considered acceptable in the specific clinical context.

# 15. Overall residual risk
All situations with any danger and potential damage identified were evaluated and the risks were reduced to its lowest possible level. The overall residual risk associated with the
medical device meets the global residual risk acceptability criteria as there are no individual residual risks.
All risks were considered acceptable and additional control measures are not practicable.
After comparing the medical benefits obtained from the use of the device with the risks, the evidence collected allows us to conclude that the expected benefits clearly outweigh the
risks and, therefore, the device constitutes added value and should be made available on the market.
Use of the product is free from unacceptable risks when using the device in accordance with the manufacturer's instructions for use. The medical device does not raise undue safety
concerns; however, the safety and performance of the device will be continually monitored through ongoing surveillance activities.
# 16. Conclusion
Based on the established risk management plan, having carried out an in-depth analysis of the risks associated with VR-CARDIO family and implementing appropriate
control actions, we can conclude that the risks are now under control. The risk management measures implemented have effectively reduced the potential risks
associated with the use of the VR-CARDIO family.
It is essential to highlight that patient safety and compliance with quality standards were taken into consideration in the management of these risks.
However, it is important to maintain ongoing monitoring and carry out regular assessments to ensure that risk control measures remain effective over time. This
proactive approach will help ensure device safety and quality during clinical practice.
