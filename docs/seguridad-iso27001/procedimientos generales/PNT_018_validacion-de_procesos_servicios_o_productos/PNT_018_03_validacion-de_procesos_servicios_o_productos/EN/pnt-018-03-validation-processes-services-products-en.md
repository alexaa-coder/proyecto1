# Aim

The objective of this procedure is to establish the general validation
policy applied by Spika Tech, hereinafter SPIKA, for the products it
supplies.

# Scope

This procedure covers everything from the definition of user
requirements to product validation. This document defines the general
scope of validation and the activities involved, although the strategy
and planning of specific activities will be included in the protocols,
and their execution and results will be documented in the corresponding
reports.

# Responsibilities and Departments Affected

It is the responsibility of all SPIKA employees to collaborate and
manage product validation activities.

The Technical Manager and the Research and Development staff are
responsible for supervising the execution and compliance of this
procedure.

# Definitions and Acronyms

- Acceptance criteria: Product specifications and
 acceptance/rejection criteria, such as the acceptable quality level,
 with an associated sampling plan, that will be necessary to make the
 decision to accept or reject a product.

- Hardware and Software Installation Qualifications: Documents
 the installation, configuration, and qualification instructions for
 hardware and software, including middleware, to demonstrate that all
 of these items can perform the functions required by the application
 software.

- Operational Qualification (OQ): establishes, by objective
 evidence, that the equipment or instrument is capable of operating
 repeatedly within the determined limits and meeting the
 specifications.

- Performance Qualification (PQ): establishes, based on
 scientific evidence, that the equipment or instrument is capable of
 delivering effective and reproducible results according to the
 approved process and product specifications.

- Validation: According to the FDA, it is "the establishment of
 documented evidence that provides a high degree of assurance that a
 specific process will consistently produce a product that meets its
 predetermined specifications and quality attributes." According to
 European standards, validation is "the obtaining of evidence, in
 accordance with Good Manufacturing Practices, that any procedure,
 process, equipment, activity, or system actually produces the intended
 result." Validation refers to processes, systems, and methods.

# Procedure

# Generalities 

Validation is the collection and evaluation of documentation from the
design phase of a process through commercial production, establishing
scientific evidence that a process is capable of ensuring product
quality.

An effective validation process contributes significantly to ensuring
product quality.

An important point to consider in the validation exercise will be the
changes. Each relevant change within the product must be reviewed and
evaluated to determine whether or not it requires revalidation, as well
as periodic quality reviews.

For each validation exercise, a validation protocol must be duly
reviewed and approved before its execution, and it must be performed by
trained personnel. The trained personnel will collect all data, as well
as any incidents or nonconformities detected, to issue a final judgment
and declare the validation exercise as satisfactory or not in the
corresponding report. Where applicable, after detecting nonconformities,
corrective actions will be initiated as necessary, and the compliance
status of the validation exercise will be assessed.

All generated documentation will be archived in a secure location in the
Quality Archive.

# Types of validation 

The following types of validation to be performed at SPIKA are listed
below; however, any exercises may be evaluated as appropriate to ensure
product quality:

- Software validation

- Development tool validation

- Validation of production processes

# Methodology

Newly created products, change controls, or even quality reviews or risk
analyses will give rise to validations.

# Software

The minimum content that the Computerized Systems Validation Protocol
must present is described below:

- Aim.

- Responsibilities.

- Scope.

- Definitions.

- Staff training.

- Study of critical stages and phases

- Methodology.

- Related documentation.

- Annexes.

The software in question must be studied using Risk Analysis and at
least the following verifications must be carried out:

- Functional specifications

- Risk Analysis

- Design qualification: Since SPIKA products are developed internally,
 it must be established that functional specifications correspond to
 operational requirements and that expectations have been met.

- Installation Qualification (IQ): The installation path and the manner
 in which the software in question is installed must be qualified.

- Operational Qualification (OQ): where it must be demonstrated that the
 planned functionalities have been implemented and that they work.

- Performance Qualification (PQ): where the expected results must be
 verified through actual executions of the application.

# Development tool

The minimum content that the software development tool validation
protocol must present is described below:

- Introduction

- Scope

- Definitions

- Validation requirements

- Validation plan

- Validation procedures

- Results and conclusions

This framework will ensure complete and rigorous validation of the
software development tool, ensuring that it meets the standards required
for effective and secure use in its intended context.

# Production processes 

Process validation requires that the process is carried out using
qualified facilities, systems, and equipment, that the corresponding
methods have been validated, that work procedures are in place, and that
personnel have been trained in these procedures.

The validation strategy is reflected in the specific validation
protocol. Acceptance criteria must be defined in advance, documented,
and justified in the validation protocols, based on the results of the
AR prepared in each case.

Process validation must be completed prior to product distribution and
sale.

A corresponding validation protocol will be established for each
process, defining the type of validation, the number of batches to be
validated, the number and quantity of samples to be collected, and the
analyses to be performed. If statistically, a result of one or two
batches to be validated is obtained, the validation must be performed
considering three batches.

In the concurrent and prospective validation approach, process
validation is typically carried out with a number of consecutive batches
established based on the results of the AR. Deviations from this system
may be permitted under justified circumstances.

Likewise, a validation may concern only the manufacturing or packaging
phase because the modification requiring validation only affects one of
the phases. This will be clearly defined in the corresponding validation
protocol.

They all have a common format in which the following points are defined:

- Aim.

- Responsibilities.

- Scope.

- Teams.

- Definitions.

- Staff training.

- Study of critical stages and phases (Risk Analysis).

- Process(es) for Validation: Manufacturing and Packaging, Manufacturing
 or Packaging.

- Development of Validation.

- Environmental conditions during process validation.

- Waste Management.

# Documentation management

The protocols will be coded as follows:

- **PV-XXX/YY.ww** , where XXX is a consecutive number, YY the year of
 issue and ww the version (always starting with 01).

Validation reports will be coded as follows:

- **IV-XXX/YY.ww** , where XXX is a consecutive number, YY the year of
 issue and ww the version (always starting with 01).

For every protocol, review by the quality unit and approval by at least
the responsible technician will be required. Then, as many figures as
the validation exercise requires will be involved.

# Re-validation

Validation is not a one-time activity, but rather a continuous one. To
achieve this, a system must be defined that detects unplanned deviations
from the designed process, thus enabling control to be maintained or
improved and process and product variability to be reduced.

Continuous process verification through monitoring and trend analysis
will determine whether process changes are necessary. All of these
efforts should identify process variability and identify potential
improvements.

To ensure that processes and related procedures are robust and that
product quality is consistent, periodic process revalidation or
continuous process verification must be performed. This ongoing process
verification must be applied throughout the product lifecycle to support
the validated status of the product. The Organization's Change Control
tool and the conclusions of the Management Review Report will be used as
a basis, and an annual evaluation will be made of whether or not
revalidation is appropriate.
